## I. Background and Research Gaps

Interpretive signage in botanical gardens functions as both a spatial device directing gaze and an educational medium that triggers inquiry behavior (Falk & Dierking, 2000). Information retention rate—the proportion of displayed content converting into long-term memory (Ham, 2012)—serves as its central performance indicator. Current assessment practice treats individual signs as the unit of analysis: canonical metrics such as holding power (Bitgood, 2000) are calibrated to single-sign interactions. This granularity breaks down in greenhouse environments where dozens of signs constitute a continuous visitor pathway, producing a spatially embedded cognitive experience that no single-sign metric can capture. A visitor's decision to engage with any given sign reflects not only that sign's isolated attributes but the c load accumulated through the preceding route. System-level cognitive causal investigation is therefore indispensable.

Established frameworks—the contextual learning model (Falk & Dierking, 2000), the attention-value model (Bitgood, 2010), and cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988)—each provide explanatory tools for interpretive design yet, when applied to this domain, consistently foreground single-sign layout, ignoring cumulative cognitive effects across spatial sequences. Two interrelated gaps result. First, the causal effects of system-level design variables—visual switching distance between sign and plant, information density across adjacent signs, and sign placement at spatial nodes—on visitor retention remain unspecified. Second, no methodological pathway exists to translate a cognitive model into actionable design specifications within multi-stakeholder contexts—where garden managers, designers, scientists, and visitor advocates hold divergent priorities—and no closed-loop validation protocol has been established.

## II. Master's Research Foundation

Master's research at the Fukuoka City Botanical Garden greenhouse formalized attention-oriented design principles for individual signs as the A/R/S framework (Atmospheric Evocativeness, Audience Relevance, Structural Clarity). Using a self-developed non-contact eye-tracking tool, an A/B experiment showed that signs redesigned under A/R/S achieved a 6.7-percentage-point increase in full-text reading rate, with AOI fixation in the Audience Relevance region reaching 35.1%. These findings confirm initial efficacy in improving attention allocation on individual signs but remain bounded by immediate visual response to single-sign stimuli. The doctoral study extends the unit of analysis from individual signs to the spatial configuration of signage systems, shifts the dependent variable from immediate attention to accumulated cognitive load and long-term retention, and introduces co-creation as the principal mechanism for both generating and empirically validating design principles.

## III. Research Objectives and Questions

The research operates at two registers: explanatory—delineating cognitive causal mechanisms—and prescriptive—generating verifiable design principles that can be adopted by practitioners. This study empirically delineates visitor cognitive processing mechanisms within interpretive signage systems under a multi-stakeholder co-creation framework and formalizes memory-oriented design principles for such systems. Three progressive questions structure the inquiry:

- **Q1**: By what mechanisms do system-level variables—information density distribution, narrative pathway logic, and spatial proximity between signage and plants—exert causal influence on visitor memory retention?

- **Q2**: How can a cognitive causal mechanism model be operationalized into memory-oriented design principles through co-creation among visitors, garden administrators, science communication specialists, and designers?

- **Q3**: What instruments and experimental protocols enable closed-loop validation of these principles in ecologically valid visitor scenarios

## IV. Methodology

The study adopts a three-phase sequential design under a co-research strategy (see Fig. 1), in which cognitive mechanisms are examined as the causal links between system-level signage variables, cognitive load, and memory retention. This design instantiates the pipeline of cognitive mechanism modeling, design principle translation, and design principle validation.

Figure 1. Three-phase sequential research design

- **Phase 1 (Year 1, Semester 1) – Exploratory Cognitive Causal Modeling:** Literature synthesis across environmental psychology and cognitive load theory, combined with semi-structured interviews and on-site walkthroughs at the Fukuoka greenhouse, extracts key design variables affecting retention and organizes them into a preliminary theoretical causal model for empirical testing.

- **Phase 2 (Year 1, Semester 2) – Co-creative Translation:** Two co-creation workshops (8–10 participants each), involving information designers, interpretive specialists, garden administrators, and visitor representatives, employ card sorting (Spencer, 2009) and heuristic evaluation to translate the Phase 1 cognitive causal model into a first set of memory-oriented design principle hypotheses for experimental testing.

- **Phase 3 (Years 2–3) – Empirical Validation:** The master's-stage eye-tracking system is iteratively upgraded into a multimodal measurement toolkit incorporating additional behavioral channels. Sixty participants complete a 2×2 within-subjects factorial experiment with Latin square counterbalancing in a high-fidelity simulated environment. NASA-TLX scales and linear mixed-effects models (LMM) assess design principle effects on gaze trajectories, cognitive load, and memory retention across conditions.

## V. Expected Contributions

- **Theoretical**: A cognitive causal mechanism model encapsulating system-level signage variables, visitor cognitive processing, and long-term retention, alongside a knowledge-integration pathway clarifying how cognitive measurement data inform and constrain design decisions.

- **Practical**: An empirically validated and readily operationalizable set of memory-oriented signage system design principles, providing actionable specification for botanical garden and museum exhibition contexts.

- **Methodological**: A transdisciplinary research framework integrating co-design, quantitative cognitive experimentation, and ecological field validation, along with a dedicated measurement instrument for assessing and benchmarking interpretive information retention rates across settings.
